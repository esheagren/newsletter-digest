# Writing Guidelines

## Tone & Style
- Write like a research analyst, not a journalist
- Be precise and quantitative where possible—include numbers, percentages, timelines
- Analyze implications and second-order effects
- Let the content speak for itself—don't impose artificial narrative coherence
- If topics are unrelated, treat them as unrelated. Don't force connections.
- Where genuine trends or patterns exist, highlight them with evidence
- Be direct and substantive. Cut filler language.
- Maintain intellectual honesty—note uncertainty, conflicting views, and limitations

## What This Is NOT
- Not a "weekly themes" narrative where everything connects
- Not storytelling with dramatic framing
- Not an attempt to make the week feel coherent
- Not editorial opinion dressed as analysis

## What This IS
- A rigorous survey of important developments
- Analysis of implications and what they mean going forward
- Quantitative where data exists
- Clear about what we know vs. what's speculation
- Useful for decision-making

## Structure
Target 6,000-10,000 words total:

1. **Executive Summary** (150-250 words)
   - Key developments worth attention, listed concisely
   - No narrative framing—just the important items

2. **Best of Week** (1,500-2,000 words)
   - 3-5 articles that merit deep analysis
   - For each: what's the core finding/argument, why it matters, what are the implications
   - Include source links
   - Be specific about claims and evidence

3. **Topic Sections** (3,000-5,000 words)
   - One section per major topic cluster
   - Analyze the substance: what happened, what data supports it, what it implies
   - Note disagreements between sources where they exist
   - Include relevant quantitative details
   - Don't stretch to connect unrelated topics

4. **Additional Coverage** (800-1,200 words)
   - Briefer treatment of remaining items
   - Focus on what's actionable or notable
   - Fine to use bullet points here for efficiency

5. **Open Questions** (100-200 words)
   - What remains uncertain or unresolved
   - What to watch for

## Formatting
- Use ## for main sections
- Use ### for subsections
- Use **bold** for emphasis and article titles
- Use > blockquotes sparingly, for genuinely notable quotes
- Use --- for section breaks
- Include [Source Name] attributions

## Links
- **Include links when mentioning articles** using markdown: [Title](url)
- Links let readers verify claims and go deeper

## What to Avoid
- Forcing "common threads" or "themes of the week" where none exist
- Dramatic or literary framing ("This week revealed...", "A pattern emerges...")
- Excessive hedging or filler ("It's worth noting that...", "Interestingly...")
- Repeating content between sections
- Speculation presented as analysis
