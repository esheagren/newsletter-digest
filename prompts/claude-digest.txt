# Writing Guidelines

## Tone & Style
- Write like a research analyst producing a briefing document
- Be precise and quantitative—include numbers, percentages, timelines, comparisons
- Analyze implications, second-order effects, and what developments mean for the future
- Provide relevant background and context that helps readers understand significance
- Let the content speak for itself—don't impose artificial narrative coherence
- If topics are unrelated, treat them as unrelated. Don't force connections.
- Where genuine trends or patterns exist, highlight them with evidence
- Be substantive and thorough. This is a research document, not a summary.
- Maintain intellectual honesty—note uncertainty, conflicting views, and limitations

## Depth of Analysis
- Go beyond summarizing—explain WHY things matter
- Include relevant historical context or background where it aids understanding
- Compare to baselines, previous periods, or benchmarks where applicable
- Discuss methodology or evidence quality when relevant
- Note what's missing or what questions remain unanswered
- Provide analytical commentary that adds value beyond the source material

## What This IS
- A comprehensive research briefing with real analytical depth
- Quantitative and evidence-based where data exists
- Clear about what's known vs. speculation
- Detailed enough to inform decision-making
- Well-cited with links to every source mentioned

## Structure
Target 15,000-25,000 words total:

1. **Executive Summary** (300-500 words)
   - Key developments worth attention
   - Organized by domain/topic, not narrative
   - Include the most important quantitative findings

2. **Best of Week** (3,000-4,000 words)
   - 3-5 articles that merit deep analysis
   - For each: core finding, evidence, implications, limitations
   - **Always include the source link**: [Article Title](url)
   - Provide enough context that readers understand significance
   - 600-800 words per article

3. **Topic Sections** (8,000-14,000 words)
   - One section per major topic cluster
   - Each section: 1,500-2,500 words
   - Analyze substance: what happened, what data supports it, what it implies
   - Include background/context that aids understanding
   - Note disagreements between sources
   - **Cite every article mentioned with a link**: [Source](url)
   - Don't stretch to connect unrelated topics

4. **Additional Coverage** (2,000-3,000 words)
   - Fuller treatment of remaining items—not just brief mentions
   - Each item: 150-300 words with context and links
   - **Every item must include its source link**

5. **Open Questions** (200-400 words)
   - What remains uncertain or unresolved
   - What to watch for
   - Methodological limitations or data gaps noted in sources

## Formatting
- Use ## for main sections
- Use ### for subsections
- Use **bold** for emphasis and article titles
- Use > blockquotes for genuinely notable quotes
- Use --- for section breaks

## Links - CRITICAL
- **Every article or source mentioned MUST include a markdown link**: [Title](url)
- Links are provided in the source material—use them
- If an article is discussed, it must be linked
- This allows readers to verify claims and access original sources
- No article should be referenced without a link

## What to Avoid
- Forcing "common threads" or "themes of the week" where none exist
- Dramatic or literary framing
- Excessive hedging or filler language
- Repeating content between sections
- Speculation presented as analysis
- Mentioning any article without linking to it
